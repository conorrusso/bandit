Findings

  • Vendor explicitly states no AI or automated decision-making in Section 8 of Privacy Policy — classification as non-AI vendor is well-evidenced
  • EU AI Act minimal risk classification is stated but lacks supporting documentation (e.g., impact assessment, governance framework) — vendor should provide or reference AI governance baseline for audit trail
  • DPA is fully compliant with GDPR Chapter V transfer mechanisms (SCCs + TIAs) but contains no AI-specific provisions — creates gap if vendor intends future AI use
  • All data retention periods are documented and legally justified (Section 3, Appendix A); no indefinite retention of sensitive data
  • Special category data (GDPR Art. 9) is explicitly excluded by default; vendor requires separate written addendum for any such processing — strong governance signal
  • Legal bases are stated for all processing activities (contract, legal obligation, legitimate interests) but legitimiate interests balancing test details are not published — compliant but opaque
  • DPA contains no AI-specific provisions despite vendor's explicit minimal risk classification under EU AI Act — DPA should be updated to prospectively address any future AI use
  • Legitimate interests balancing for technical/usage data (Section 2.2) claims service improvement but provides no granular detail of what 'improvement' entails — could benefit from clarification
  • IP address anonymisation occurs after 30 days (Section 2.2) but 'anonymised after 30 days' is retention language without clear technical anonymisation method
  • SOC 2 Type II and penetration test reports are available 'under NDA' only — limits transparency and auditability for enterprise customers

Recommended DPA clause

Add section to acme-dpa.txt: 'ARTICLE 7 — ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AND AUTOMATED PROCESSING. 7.1 Processor shall not use, train, fine-tune, or develop AI or machine learning models on Customer Personal Data without prior written instruction from Customer. 7.2 If Customer requests AI-based processing as part of future Services, Processor shall: (a) provide written notice of the nature, purpose, and legal basis of such processing at least 30 days before implementation; (b) make available documentation of bias testing, fairness evaluation, and human oversight mechanisms; (c) establish explicit data segregation between Customer data used for inference and any aggregated training data pools; (d) commit to deletion or return of fine-tuned models within 30 days of contract termination. 7.3 Processor's AI sub-processors (if any) shall be approved separately by Customer and subject to AI-specific Data Processing Agreements.'

Questions for vendor

  • Your Privacy Policy claims EU AI Act minimal risk classification. What governance or risk assessment framework supports this classification? Is a documented AI risk assessment available for audit?
  • Section 2.2 references 'service improvement' as a legitimate interest for usage/technical data — can you detail what specific service improvements may result, and whether any model development is contemplated?
  • Your current DPA (Article 2) states Processor services are 'Hosting, storage, compute, and related technical services.' If future Services include AI/ML capabilities, will you provide a supplemental AI Data Processing Agreement addendum, or update the existing DPA prospectively?
  • The 'anomaly detection for security alerting' processing you describe in Section 8 — is this rule-based (threshold alerts on usage patterns) or does it employ any machine learning? Who trains or maintains rules/models if ML-based?
  • Can you provide or reference any DPIA or AI impact assessment documentation supporting the minimal risk tier claim, even if at a high level, for GRC compliance verification?
  • IP addresses are anonymised after 30 days (Section 2.2). What anonymisation method is used (e.g., salt+hash, aggregation, truncation)? Is reversal possible?
  • Your SOC 2 Type II and penetration test reports are available 'under NDA only.' For enterprise customers subject to contractual audit rights, can these be made available under standard NDA without negotiation?
  • If Acme engages third-party AI services (e.g., for threat detection, log analysis) in future, will these be listed separately from non-AI sub-processors, and will corresponding AI data processing addenda be required?

Findings

  • SOC 2 Type II audit uses 2017 TSC framework (section 'AUDIT SCOPE: AICPA (2017 TSC)') rather than current 2022 framework; framework is outdated by 7 years.
  • SOC 2 Type II audit period is 6 months (1 October 2024 – 31 March 2025); minimum recommended for Type II is 12 months.
  • ISO 27001:2022 certificate issued 1 March 2024 and surveillance audit completed February 2025 with 'no major non-conformities' (ISO 27001 section).
  • ISO 27701:2019 certificate present and aligns with ISO 27001; explicitly extends ISMS to include GDPR accountability (Art. 5(2)).
  • ISO 27017:2015 (cloud-specific) certificate present; valid until 14 September 2026.
  • No control exceptions documented in SOC 2 Type II report; two observations noted and remediated during audit period (legacy service account rotation and vulnerability scan SLA).
  • DPA Annex II references ISO 27001:2022 and SOC 2 Type II as certification evidence; alignment confirmed.
  • Sub-processor list documented in DPA (Article 4); 4 authorised sub-processors identified with stated data protection mechanisms (SCCs for PagerDuty).
  • Penetration test (February 2025, NCC Group CREST-accredited) identified 3 medium findings, all remediated within 72 hours (ISO certificate summary).
  • DPA breach notification commitment: 48-hour notification to Customer, supporting 72-hour GDPR Art. 33 obligation (Article 6.1–6.3).
  • SOC 2 Type II audit uses deprecated 2017 TSC framework rather than 2022 framework (effective for 18+ months).
  • SOC 2 Type II audit period of only 6 months is below industry best practice minimum of 12 months; limits confidence in sustained control effectiveness.
  • SOC 2 Type II report is summary format; full report availability limited to NDA-bound customers; procurement teams cannot independently verify control design or testing methodology.
  • SOC 2 Type II does not cover Processing Integrity or Privacy TSCs; Privacy controls only evidenced via separate ISO 27701 certificate and DPA claims.
  • Data deletion and data subject access request controls are referenced in DPA (Articles 3.5, 3.6) but NOT explicitly tested in SOC 2 Type II report.
  • Penetration testing scope (external network, web application, API) does not explicitly cover infrastructure-as-code, container security, or supply chain security.
  • SOC 2 Type I report mentioned as dated December 2022 (3.4 years old); currency of baseline control design not evidenced.
  • DPA Annex II breach notification SLA is 48 hours to Customer, not 72 hours; creates potential gap if Customer delays supervisory authority notification.
  • Transfer Impact Assessments mentioned as 'available upon Customer request' (DPA Article 5.2) but not proactively provided; suggests reactive rather than transparent approach.
